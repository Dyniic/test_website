5 Sustainability Tips

Bailey Hirota


JULY 28, 2019

It’s no secret that the environment is in danger. Prevent further degradation by switching to a more sustainable lifestyle. Small changes, although seemingly negligible, can contribute to great change over sustained periods of time. We only have one earth; we should do our best to return it to an abundant, vibrant state as soon as possible. And that begins with us.

Purposeful Shopping

The old saying “reduce, reuse, recycle” still holds true today. Every product has an “environmental footprint,” which includes the materials needed to create it to the pollution emitted during the manufacturing process to the packaging that is eventually relocated to landfills. Before buying something, ask yourself, “Do I really need this?”. Ensuring each purchase is necessary will both save money and reduce environmental footprint.

Make sure big purchases have big environmental benefits

When car shopping, consider fuel-efficient models - not only will you save thousands in gas money, but you will also drastically reduce your carbon footprint over the years. If you’re looking to buy a refrigerator, washer, dryer, or microwave, be aware of Energy Star labels to help you find the most efficient, energy saving appliances. For new water heaters, upgrade to solar.

Go plastic free - #standagainstplastic

With a 1000 year decomposition time, plastic never really just 'goes away.' Today, it accounts for just over 40% of the world's ocean surface. Numerous marine mammals are killed after ingesting or being impaled by plastic. Implementing plastic-free habits in your daily life, such as using reusable shopping bags, water bottles, and straws, and supporting brands that are sustainable, can help to reduce plastic pollution.

Pay attention to labels

Similar to #3, buying goods that are Fair Trade, organic, or "shade-grown" can contribute to a more sustainable lifestyle. "Shade-grown" coffee is grown while keeping forest habitats intact for migratory birds and other species. Fair Trade certified goods support companies dedicated to sustainable production and fair wages. Organic foods help to keep harmful pesticides out, protecting workers, wildlife, and the environment.

Mindful Eating

Meat production is undoubtedly one of the most environmentally destructive industries on the planet. It is also a major contributor to pollution, greenhouse gas emissions, and habitat destruction. Each day, we can contribute to a reduced environmental footprint - and each time we eat, we can choose to improve the health of the planet by reducing our meat intake. In addition, around 40% of edible food in the US is wasted. Smart, planned shopping and a well devised meal plan can ensure complete use of the foods you purchase.

Thirty-three public schools in Howard County were recognized for mold outbreaks in locker rooms, gyms, and athletic storage closets. This wasn’t a first - in 2016, there were exactly twelve Howard County schools that had higher level molds than what was expected. Now, the complaints from students and faculty have rolled in once again.

The main sources of the outbreaks are athletic departments, like locker rooms, and sports gear, such as helmets, volleyballs, and swim caps. Some schools involved are Ellicott Mills Middle School, Lime Kiln Middle School, River Hill High School, Hammond Elementary, and Northfield Elementary.

Mold growing is a definite health hazard. The effects of mold include asthma, allergic reactions, and irritation in the eyes, nose, throat, lungs, and skin. Health guideline officials have taken notice and have investigated janitors and staff members that may be held responsible for involvement in the outbreak. For containment, strict procedures have been established. First, IEQ officials review each school by doing walk-throughs. Then, the Office of Environment contacts each official who submits a concern, and lastly, a school hygienist drops ceilings for a further and more in depth walk-through.

Through this, school officials have concluded that the thermostat created the problem - individuals would change the temperature frequently without realizing the implications. Keeping a constant temperature and deep cleaning affected areas are core elements of the solution to the outbreak. If it isn’t fixed, students will face health risks from the exposed fungi.

Our schools are important too. We need to focus on the health of not only students, but also the school property itself. Keeping gyms, bathrooms, and classrooms clean will allow a more positive, fresh, and healthy environment for the students.

California’s First Step Towards Eliminating Plastic Straws


Collin Kelso


AUGUST 10, 2019

On September 20, 2018, Democratic Assemblyman Ian Calderon achieved California’s first step towards eradicating plastic straws when a bill outlining their statewide restriction was passed. This bill, California AB-1884, stated that sit-down restaurants were banned from providing customers with plastic straws except on request. While this bill may seem mundane, it is extremely important for the environment. That begs the question: why is this recent bill so important?

The answer is simple: a reduction in plastic straws would drastically reduce waste and massively improve the welfare of Earth’s oceans. According to Money.com, some scientists estimate that over seven million straws pollute U.S. shorelines alone. These straws lodge themselves into marine ecosystems, directly harming plants and animals in the process. Calderon directly cites this as the primary motive behind this law; he states that “we need to create awareness around the issue of one-time use plastic straws and its detrimental effects on our landfills, waterways, and oceans.” To put this legislation into effect, Calderon implemented steep penalties for businesses that did not adhere to it. According to the California Legislature, a fine of $25 will be incurred for every day that a violation is committed. While this bill aims to reduce the usage of plastic straws, it is not intended to be an outright ban; Calderon comments that “it is a small step towards curbing our reliance on these convenience products, which will hopefully contribute to a change in customer attitudes and usage.” Various towns and cities, including Davis, Calif. and San Luis Obispo, Calif., have already passed legislation to lower the prevalence of plastic straws. However, California AB-1884 affects the entire state, which allows statewide efforts to be made against plastic straws and other pollutants.

The provisions and motives backing California AB-1884 make it a highly effective law. To start, Calderon’s motives are committed to reducing the impact of plastic straws in marine environments. As this law passed with a majority vote, it shows that Californian officials share this sentiment. To back these motives, California AB-1884 imposes steep fines for violators, which encourages businesses to phase out straws or consider an alternative. Both of these factors encourage the state of California to reduce their plastic straw usage. While this law is not a fully comprehensive solution, one thing is certain: it is a step in the right direction.

Secondly, through the cycle ocean water is deacidified as the sulfate ions that are released help neutralize some of the ocean acidity caused by ocean acidification with CO2. A Pressing Matter: the Dangers in the Gulf of Mexico’s Dead Zone


Evan H and Charan K

January 29th 2020

The Dead Zone, an area devoid of life, covers over 6,000 square miles in the Gulf of Mexico, a sector practically the size of New Jersey.

This area sits between the delta of the Mississippi river and the upper Texas Coast, but it is not the only one. There are many others scattered across the globe in areas such as the Baltic Sea and Lake Erie.

What is a dead zone? A dead zone is an area in a large body of water where there is practically no oxygen. As a result, any life form requiring oxygen that makes the mistake of venturing into such zones is met with instant death.

What causes dead zones to form? Primarily, farming practices that add an overabundance of nutrients into the soil have been a major contributor to these dead zones. Nutrient runoff from states stretching from Wisconsin down to Mississippi feed into the Mississippi River. With the addition of nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus, there has been a rapid growth in algae. This alarming growth depletes dissolved oxygen.

Since we know what is causing the issues in the Gulf of Mexico what can we do to fix them? First, we know that fertilizer is a large contributor to the dead zones. By reducing the amount of fertilizer and adjusting the timing of its application seasonally, we can limit the runoff of excess nutrients from the farmlands. The major issue is that many farmers do not know about the problems fertilizers bring and focus on the production and growth of their crops. Along with that, they do not really control animal wastes that are excreted on their farms. Animal wastes have organic matter such as proteins, carbohydrates, and nitrates, which flow into waterways and result in issues such as dead zones. Additionally, the industrial industry is a large contributor to the problem at hand. Through careful monitoring and practices, such as limiting the discharge of nutrients, chemicals, and organic matter from manufacturing facilities and farmlands, we can focus on improving the Gulf of Mexico in the future.

If You Want to Prevent Climate Change, Start in the Arctic


Abhay Rao

February 12th 2020

An area that most of us will probably never visit is probably the most important zone in the world for preventing climate change. A study published in Geopolitical Research Letters found that losing the Arctic’s reflective summer ice would be equivalent to an extra 25 years of emissions, pushing us past the point of no return. In order to tackle this complicated issue, let’s explore the problems and solutions that pertain to this critical area.

Of course, protecting this all-important ice layer involves reducing CO2 emissions, but that is not enough. Super-pollutants, such as black carbon, methane, and hydrofluorocarbon refrigerants are multiple times more potent than carbon dioxide. Strict regulations are required to make sure that these damaging substances do not reach the Arctic - California provides a prime example where they were able to cut black carbon emissions by 90% by using strict guidelines. California is also legislating limits on methane released from landfills and strict regulations on refrigerants used in consumer products.

Innovation is also providing new solutions to the Arctic crisis. A non-profit called Ice911 plans to cover ice with a type of sand to increase the reflectivity of Arctic ice. Although these plans seem to be effective and risk-free, they have not been implemented yet. In order to protect the all-important ice, innovative solutions such as these should be rapidly tested and eventually rolled out on a large scale. An even more innovative approach is to insert sulfates into the atmosphere to reflect solar radiation, although implementation of this is far off.

Now is the time to take action towards saving the Arctic. Winning this battle is the only way that we have a chance to win the war against climate change.

The Great Lakes are Rising too Fast


Abhay Rao

February 26th 2020

The people of the city of Chicago live with a dangerous neighbor. The neighboring Great Lakes keep rising as they are entering a new era of volatility. The last five years have been the wettest in history for the Great Lakes watershed, which encompasses eight states.

This rising threat has not gone unnoticed. The city of Chicago has installed flood barriers surrounding lakefronts, while New York plans to spend $300 million on protective measures to reduce flooding along the south shore of Lake Ontario. The Governor of Wisconsin has asked President Trump to designate the areas damaged by Lake Michigan’s flooding a federal disaster zone.

For people who live in these communities, it isn’t as simple as signing a bill. Communities are making decisions about how to protect themselves from a potential catastrophe, but this will come at a cost to taxpayers. Particularly, older areas have built their homes far too close to water. Although they were able to survive during times of low water levels and the flooding of the 1980s, it seems unlikely they will be able to continue forward as weather patterns change.

The strife that these communities are in is directly caused by climate change. As temperatures increase due to human-caused warming, the atmosphere can hold more moisture, causing severe storms. These events have drenched the Great Lakes region throughout the past summer and fall.

When discussing issues such as climate change, we often get carried away thinking about long term goals that seem unattainable now. However, global warming is having serious, concrete effects in communities close to us, which is why it is more important than ever to do everything we can to prevent future crises.






The Current Mining Situation


Evan H and Charan K

March 4th 2020

Mining needs to become more environmentally-friendly. Although the global economy depends on these mined resources, mining only serves as a temporary solution as the minerals mined are non-renewable resources.

Although preserving the environment is one of the top priorities now, that was not always the case. Over the past two centuries, scientists that developed new mining technologies never gave environmental sustainability any consideration. Mining poses a threat to nearby bodies of water. As soil erosion increases due to the loosening of soil caused by mining, sediment can collect in streambeds, “suffocating” the wildlife present. In addition, the waste from mines is acidic and can lower the overall PH of the body of water significantly, devastating the wildlife present.

Wildlife is not the only one affected by mining. Mining loosens soil and encourages erosion. Many of these particles become airborne and can contain many toxic substances used in mining such as cadmium. These particles have linked to many adverse health effects on people and animals, impacting the ecosystems and our interaction with them.

The health effects range from respiratory tract diseases to cardiovascular diseases and asthma. Other diseases caused by unsafe water include cholera, giardia, and typhoid. Chemicals such as arsenic, pesticides, and nitrates can mix into the water, putting infants and pregnant women at the greatest risk. Water-borne pathogens also pose a risk to us. To bluntly put into perspective, water pollution killed 1.8 million people in 2015 and sickens a billion people annually. Furthermore, the people mostly getting affected are low-income citizens who live near industrial sites that cause the pollution. However, even wealthy first-world countries such as the United States struggle with keeping its water supply clean. Thousands of Legionnaire disease cases spring up every year in the U.S.

It is known that ecosystems are an intricate web consisting of animals, plants, fungi, bacteria, and interactions between biotic and abiotic things. By disrupting any of these, the web that was once created in a sturdy fashion will topple, impacting the ecosystem as a whole.






Pollution in the face of COVID-19


Sanjay Nagaraj

March 17th 2020

As a result of sweeping regulations due to the prevalence of the novel coronavirus, CO2 emissions and greenhouse gases in the atmosphere have decreased drastically. The European Space Agency monitored concentrations of NO2 or nitrogen dioxide, and between Jan 1 and March 12, areas of Italy that were hit harder by COVID-19 were correlated with lower nitrogen dioxide levels. This was obviously caused by a larger decline in car traffic in the harder hit areas. Although this drop in emissions may seem promising, experts warn that this drop will only last as long as the world economies are depressed, which seems temporary. Italy's drop in emissions is promising for other countries in the EU and around the world who can hopefully follow their lead in instituting reforms and making climate change education mandatory in schools, to improve awareness and citizen involvement in preserving the environment.
